# Composite — Recurring Patterns (draft 03)

## A. Composite Profile

> **Fictionalized anonymized composite.** This profile does not describe a
> real person. It is a fictional illustration of patterns that recurred
> across multiple interviews.

Nobody in his family would have called it instability. It was just how
things were: a grandfather nobody discussed, an uncle who disappeared for
stretches and came back thinner, a parent who managed, mostly, until she
didn't. He grew up watching adults improvise around absences — of money, of
explanation, of each other — and he learned early that you don't ask, you
adapt. The adaptations were not mistakes. They were intelligent answers to
the house he actually lived in: stay alert, keep your own counsel, tell
yourself whatever story lets you get up tomorrow, and take relief wherever
relief is sold. Every one of those skills protected him. That is the part
he needs people to understand first, because it is the part that explains
everything after.

The drinking and the pills didn't feel like a decision — they felt like
the same adaptation, working again. The first thing they ever did was
work: the dread went quiet, the body unclenched, the room felt survivable.
And then, somewhere in his twenties, the protection changed jobs without
telling him. Work existed to fund it. Friendships narrowed to the people
who shared it. The cheaper the supply, the further he followed it, into
apartments and parking lots and corners of town where being hurt was a
known cost of doing business. He was hurt there more than once, and he
kept going back — not because he couldn't see the harm, but because going
back had saved him before, and a thing that has saved you does not feel
like a threat. It feels like home.

The system met him piecemeal. A handful of arrests, a warrant he ignored
until it couldn't be ignored, a short stretch in county somewhere in the
blur of those years. Steady work never survived that period either; jobs
ended quietly, for reasons tangled up with everything else. A judge sent
him to a program once; his family begged him into another. He completed
both the way you complete a sentence — compliant, counting days, absorbing
nothing that touched the actual problem. Each time he walked out, he
walked back to the same relief, the same people, the same corner of the
same logic, and each return felt less like a failure than like a homing
instinct. Other people called it a cycle. From inside, it was simpler
than that: no one had ever asked him to choose, and he had never once told
himself that a choice existed. The programs demanded compliance. The
courts demanded compliance. Compliance was just one more adaptation, and
he was fluent in it.

What finally moved was small, and it was grammatically different from
everything before it. One ordinary morning he heard himself ask someone
for help — not to get anyone off his back, not to shorten a sentence, but
because for the first time he framed the thing as his to decide. He
describes it as the first honest sentence he'd said in years: not a
promise, not a surrender to someone else's plan, just the acknowledgment
that a decision existed and belonged to him. What followed was slow and
unglamorous. He stopped answering the old phone numbers, stopped being
available to the people and places that ran on the old logic, and found
that lonely was survivable in a way he hadn't believed. He had to retire
the protections one at a time, the way you'd thank an old guard dog and
stop feeding it — the numbing first, then the vigilance, then, hardest of
all, the stories. The shame took longest. Shame had been the engine the
whole time — every harm he'd done proving what he already believed about
himself, every proof requiring more anesthetic. Learning to hold what he'd
done without being annihilated by it was, he says, the actual work. He
doesn't call himself a victim, though he can name the people and systems
that failed him. He keeps a plain, private faith that he is reluctant to
explain. What he says about the years he lost is not that he was weak, but
that he was loyal — to defenses that had earned it once — and that getting
well began the day the loyalty question became a choice he knew he was
making.
