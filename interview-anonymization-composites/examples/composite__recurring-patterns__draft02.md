# Composite — Recurring Patterns (draft 02)

## A. Composite Profile

> **Fictionalized anonymized composite.** This profile does not describe a
> real person. It is a fictional illustration of patterns that recurred
> across multiple interviews.

Nobody in his family would have called it instability. It was just how
things were: a grandfather nobody discussed, an uncle who disappeared for
stretches and came back thinner, a parent who managed, mostly, until she
didn't. He grew up watching adults improvise around absences — of money, of
explanation, of each other — and he learned early that you don't ask, you
adapt. When people later asked where the trouble started, he never had a
clean answer. There was no single disaster. There was a household where
distress was ordinary and no one had ever been taught what to do with it,
and there was him, carrying a low hum of dread he assumed everyone carried.

The drinking and the pills didn't feel like a decision. They felt like
relief, and then like maintenance, and then — somewhere in his twenties,
he could never say exactly when — like the organizing principle of his
life. Work existed to fund it. Friendships narrowed to the people who
shared it. The cheaper the supply, the further he followed it, into
apartments and parking lots and corners of town where being hurt was a
known cost of doing business. He was hurt there more than once. He kept
going back, because the arithmetic of dependence doesn't weigh danger the
way other people's arithmetic does.

The system met him piecemeal. A handful of arrests, a warrant he ignored
until it couldn't be ignored, a short stretch in county somewhere in the
blur of those years. Steady work never survived that period either; jobs
ended quietly, for reasons tangled up with everything else. A judge sent
him to a program once; his family begged him into another. He completed
both the way you complete a sentence — compliant, counting days, absorbing
nothing that touched the actual problem. He does not say the programs were
useless. He says he wasn't in them; his body was. The help on offer was real and out of reach at the same
time: the decent places cost more than he'd earned in any year of his life,
and the places he could access felt like holding pens. What he remembers
most is how much power everyone else had — judges, caseworkers, intake
staff — and how little he had, and how none of that power ever seemed to
touch the thing that was actually wrong.

What finally moved was small. There was no dramatic bottom, or rather there
had been so many that the word stopped meaning anything. One ordinary
morning he heard himself ask someone for help — not to get anyone off his
back, not to shorten a sentence, just because he could not carry it
another day. He describes it as the first honest sentence he'd said in
years. What followed was slow and unglamorous: he stopped answering the
old phone numbers, stopped being available to the people and places that
ran on the old logic, and found that lonely was survivable in a way he
hadn't believed. The shame took longest. Shame had been the engine the
whole time — every harm he'd done proving what he already believed about
himself, every proof requiring more anesthetic. Learning to hold what he'd
done without being annihilated by it was, he says, the actual work. He
doesn't call himself a victim, though he can name the people and systems
that failed him. He keeps a plain, private faith that he is reluctant to
explain. He said he was done being a secret, and that this — being asked,
answering — was part of how he stays well.
